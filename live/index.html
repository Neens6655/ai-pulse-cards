The real AI arms race is now in silicon, not software

While the model wars dominate headlines, the structural battle has quietly moved one layer down: to who owns the chips. OpenAI is spending $18 billion to build its own accelerators with Broadcom; Broadcom, Apollo, and Blackstone are spinning up a 20GW XPU platform; and SK Hynix just raised $26.5 billion on its Wall Street debut. The bottleneck is no longer intelligence — it is manufactured silicon and the capital behind it. Abu Dhabi has positioned early, but the window for ownership stakes in this infrastructure layer is compressing fast.

The Desk
Why it mattersMGX's $49bn fund must decide now whether to own compute infrastructure or remain a downstream tenant of it.
The big pictureSovereign compute ownership becomes the defining geopolitical asset of the decade — late movers will rent what early movers built.

The full story

Three stories landed this week that, read separately, look like routine tech finance. Read together, they describe a structural reorganisation of who will own AI's physical substrate — and who will pay rent to use it.

OpenAI and Broadcom are in active negotiations over how to finance a custom AI accelerator project, code-named Nexus, with a production price tag reported at $18 billion across a 10GW buildout. This is not OpenAI buying chips from a supplier — this is OpenAI becoming a chip co-owner, internalising a capability it currently pays Nvidia billions to access. Simultaneously, Broadcom, Apollo, and Blackstone announced the AI XPV platform: a 20GW XPU compute venture backed by two of the world's largest alternative asset managers, targeting deployment by 2028. And SK Hynix — the memory supplier that sits inside every high-bandwidth chip stack — debuted on Wall Street at $170 a share, raising $26.5 billion, the largest-ever foreign company listing in the US. The market is pricing memory scarcity, not just model capability.

The obvious read is that big tech is diversifying supply chains and Wall Street is chasing the AI trade. That is true but incomplete. What is actually happening is that the AI compute stack is being vertically integrated and financially securitised at the same time. Apollo and Blackstone are not technology investors — they are infrastructure investors. They are treating gigawatts of AI compute the way they treat toll roads and pipelines: long-duration assets with captive demand and predictable yield. When infrastructure capital at that scale moves in, the ownership window for strategic investors starts to close.

For Abu Dhabi, this matters precisely because MGX just closed its inaugural Fund I at $49 billion — above its $45 billion target — and Gulf sovereign funds collectively deployed a record $53.9 billion in the first half of 2026. The capital is there. The question is what it buys. A position in the Broadcom-Apollo-Blackstone XPV platform, or a co-investment in the OpenAI-Broadcom Nexus structure, would give MGX direct exposure to the physical layer of AI — not to any single model or application, but to the substrate every model must run on. That is a categorically different risk profile than backing another software company.

The Humain-Cohere deal this week, in which Canada's Cohere partnered with the Saudi-backed Humain, signals that Gulf sovereigns are already moving to secure AI capability partnerships at the national level. But partnering for model access is not the same as owning compute. Saudi Arabia is learning this. The more durable play — the one that does not expire when a better model arrives — is to hold the infrastructure those models run on.

The specific move to watch: whether MGX or Mubadala takes a meaningful anchor position in a Western-led compute infrastructure vehicle in the next two quarters, before Apollo and Blackstone's XPV platform has closed its capital stack. Once that window shuts, Abu Dhabi goes from co-owner to customer.

How AI is transforming natural disaster prediction

AI is moving beyond the physics-based models that have long underpinned disaster forecasting, weaving together satellite feeds, sensor data, and historical records to predict floods, wildfires, and storms with far greater precision. Insurers, city planners, and emergency services are the early beneficiaries — shifting from reactive crisis management to decisions made days or weeks ahead. The real prize is lives and money: faster warnings mean communities can act before disaster strikes, not after. The sticking points are data quality, model transparency, and getting scientists, governments, and the private sector into the same room.

Financial Times
Why it mattersA region exposed to extreme heat, flash flooding, and coastal risk has every reason to be an early adopter of AI-driven disaster foresight.
The big pictureAI is quietly rewriting the economics of natural disaster risk — shifting the burden from recovery to prevention.

The full story

For decades, disaster prediction has run on physics-based simulations — powerful but slow, data-hungry, and often too coarse to catch local hazards in time. A new generation of machine-learning models is changing that by pulling in satellite imagery, IoT sensors, social-media signals, and decades of historical event data to produce sharper, faster forecasts for floods, wildfires, and major storms. The Financial Times reports that insurers are already repricing risk portfolios on the back of these tools, while city governments are using them to pre-position emergency resources and redesign evacuation plans. The upstream wins — earlier warnings, smarter infrastructure siting, lower loss ratios — flow directly into hospitals, housing, and public services. Three hard problems remain: patchy data in lower-income regions, the "black box" interpretability challenge that makes regulators nervous, and the institutional barriers between research labs, governments, and commercial players who all hold a piece of the puzzle. The direction of travel is clear; closing those gaps will determine how quickly the benefits reach the communities that need them most.

The plan's answer involves five interlocking mechanisms — algorithmic transparency, stronger competition enforcement to prevent AI monopolies, international treaty-style coordination between major powers, a fee on automated labour (effectively taxing the robot doing the job a human used to do), and a citizen's dividend paid out from the revenues that levy generates.
