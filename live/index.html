The Batch (Andrew Ng)

The big pictureAI providers are moving from single flagship models to tiered families, letting cost, safety, and task complexity drive which model actually runs.

The full story

OpenAI has previewed a new GPT-5.6 family with three distinct variants: Sol (top tier), Terra (mid tier), and Luna (fastest and least expensive). Key points:

- Access is currently limited to select U.S. government-approved organizations; a wider release is planned in the coming weeks.

- All three models include safeguards to block dangerous biological, chemical, and cybersecurity information.

- Capabilities:

- Text and image inputs with text outputs.

- Sol offers max reasoning and an ultra mode that delegates tasks to multiple agents.

- Terra and Luna provide progressively lower cost and performance.

- Prompt caching with explicit breakpoints to reuse parts of prompts.

Impact for 2026: This indicates a shift toward tiered, safety-conscious AI offerings with enhanced reasoning and multi-agent collaboration in higher-end models, alongside more affordable options, shaping how organizations adopt advanced AI capabilities shortly after a wider release.

Three things to watch amid Anthropic’s latest feud with the government

Anthropic shared its new model Mythos with a small circle of cybersecurity experts, then released a toned-down version called Fable — only for the U.S. government to slap export controls on it as a national-security threat, forcing Anthropic to pull access. The move marks one of the first times Washington has used export controls to directly constrain a commercial AI release. It signals that "AI is dangerous" rhetoric has graduated from conference panels to binding policy action.

MIT Technology Review
Why it mattersWhen Washington can freeze a model's distribution overnight, every AI lab — and every government buying their tech — needs a contingency plan.
The big pictureAI governance is shifting from voluntary safety pledges to hard regulatory levers, with export controls as the new frontier.

The full story

Summary:

- The MIT Technology Review article highlights a recent flare-up between Anthropic and the U.S. government over its AI models Mythos and Fable. Anthropic claimed Mythos could significantly aid cybersecurity, sharing access with a limited group of experts, then released a safer variant called Fable. Shortly after, the government labeled the new release a national-security threat and imposed export controls, leading Anthropic to revoke access.

- The piece frames this as part of a broader pattern where “doomers” warn of catastrophe and advocate for government intervention in AI development, with the latest episode representing tangible government action aimed at constraining a high-risk AI release.

- Three key themes to watch (as the article title suggests): (1) near-term security and misuse risks from advanced AI capable of assisting in cyber operations, (2) the effectiveness and limits of government control and export restrictions in curbing AI-enabled threats, and (3) how industry safety features and regulatory responses intersect to shape the path of high-risk AI development in 2026.
