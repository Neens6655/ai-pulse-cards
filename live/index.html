Abu Dhabi isn't just buying into AI — it's becoming the bank that funds it

Three data points from this issue, read together, tell a single story: MGX closes a $49 billion Fund I; Gulf sovereign funds post record first-half deployments; the UAE ranks 9th globally for FDI inflows at $48 billion. The obvious read is that the Gulf is flush and spending. What's actually happening is a structural role change — Abu Dhabi is no longer a passive recipient of Western AI capital, it is the primary source of patient, large-scale financing that the AI industry now depends on to get built at all.

The Desk
Why it mattersCapital leadership creates deal flow, board seats, and technology access — but only if Abu Dhabi prices that leverage correctly before the window narrows.
The big pictureWhoever controls the capital stack for frontier AI infrastructure increasingly controls the terms on which that infrastructure gets built — and used.

The full story

Three numbers sit next to each other in this issue and most readers will treat them as separate, unrelated good news. MGX closes Fund I at $49 billion, beating its $45 billion target. Gulf sovereign funds record their strongest first half ever. The UAE pulls in $48.24 billion in FDI, ranking 9th globally and first in the Middle East. Taken individually, each is a positive data point. Taken together, they describe something far more structural: Abu Dhabi has crossed from being a destination for capital into being the indispensable source of it — specifically for AI infrastructure that the West wants built but cannot fully finance alone.

The OpenAI-Broadcom story in this same issue makes the point from the other direction. A single custom chip project — Nexus, 10 gigawatts, $18 billion in production costs alone before data centre construction — is so large that two of the most capitalised technology companies in the world are still negotiating how to split the financing. The AI buildout has outrun the financing capacity of Silicon Valley. The pools of patient capital large enough to absorb tranches of this size are sovereign. They are in the Gulf.

What most observers are missing is that this is not just a financial story — it is a leverage story, and the leverage has not yet been priced. When MGX deploys at this scale, when Mubadala and ADIA follow record deployment half-years, those institutions are not buying minority stakes in exchange for returns. They are providing the float that keeps the frontier moving. The Canada-Saudi Humain-Cohere deal in this issue is a clean illustration: 50 megawatts of dedicated Gulf compute goes to Cohere in exchange for foundational model development rooted in Gulf infrastructure. Compute for capability. Capital for access. That is the template.

For Abu Dhabi specifically, the window is real but not permanent. Right now, the Gulf holds a structural advantage: sovereign balance sheets deep enough to write the cheques that close the gap between what frontier AI requires and what private markets will bear. That advantage produces not just returns but something more valuable — preferred access to models, preferential data centre placement, and the ability to shape which architectures get built where. MGX's Fund I, at $49 billion, is large enough to matter at the level of individual frontier projects, not just portfolios.

The risk is that Abu Dhabi prices this leverage as purely financial — optimising for IRR — rather than strategic. The Humain-Cohere model is instructive precisely because it demands something back that cannot be bought later: the physical embedding of frontier model training inside Gulf compute. Once a model generation is trained and deployed on infrastructure elsewhere, that moment is gone.

The decision a senior Abu Dhabi principal should be making now is not whether to deploy — that decision has been made and well executed. It is what non-financial terms to demand as a condition of each tranche. Board representation, data residency requirements, model access rights, local talent secondments, and — most consequentially — the right to host inference at scale for sovereign customers. The cheques are large enough to ask for all of it. The question is whether the ask is being made.

via The Neuron
Why it mattersGovernments designing AI policy now are effectively writing the social contract for the 2030s — and this blueprint offers a concrete template to stress-test.
The big pictureThe debate over who captures AI's gains is moving from think-pieces to concrete policy blueprints with real fiscal mechanisms.

The full story

AI 2040: Plan A is a policy report sketching an optimistic but structured path through the automation decade. Its central fiscal idea is a "robot fee" — a charge on companies that deploy AI or automation in place of human labour — with the proceeds redistributed directly to citizens as a dividend, a mechanism designed to ensure productivity gains don't pool entirely at the top.
